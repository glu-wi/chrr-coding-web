Introduction

Understanding the leading causes of death is crucial for guiding public health efforts and shaping effective policies. By identifying the conditions that contribute most significantly to mortality, public health officials can prioritize resources, design targeted interventions, and address the specific health needs of different populations. For instance, recognizing that heart disease and cancer are among the top causes of death in many countries drives initiatives focused on preventive care, early screening, and health education to reduce risk factors associated with these conditions. Analyzing leading causes of death also helps policymakers track trends over time, identify emerging health threats, and assess the effectiveness of existing health programs.

The NCHS Mortality Microdata, available at NCHS Vital Statistics Online, is a comprehensive dataset collected and maintained by the National Center for Health Statistics (NCHS). This dataset contains detailed, anonymized information on all recorded deaths in the United States, which allows researchers to study mortality trends and patterns across various demographic and geographic factors. Each death record includes an ICD-10 code that identifies the underlying cause of death. The dataset also provides demographic information about each deceased individual, including age, sex, race, and ethnicity, which enables analyses of mortality trends across different population groups. NCHS mortality data is collected annually, making it a valuable resource for analyzing trends over time.

This post builds upon a previous post, in which we created a crosswalk between ICD-10 codes and the rankable causes of death defined by the CDC. In that post, we developed a mapping that links specific ICD-10 codes to broader cause-of-death categories, simplifying the classification of mortality data for analysis. This crosswalk is helpful for working with NCHS mortality microdata in this post, as it allows us to group detailed ICD-10-coded death records into the recognized causes of death categories. In this post, we will use the crosswalk to process NCHS microdata, linking each individual ICD-10-coded cause of death to its corresponding rankable category. This approach enables us to compile and rank causes of death and identify the top 10 leading causes of death in the United States.

Setting Up the Analysis

We will us the 2022 NCHS mortality microdata, which can be downloaded from this NCHS website. A screenshot of the website is shown below, in which the 2022 file 2022 (161MB) is highlighted in yellow. It is also important to read the documentation about the data, which can be accessed by clicking on 1968-2022 under User's Guide (.pdf files). Or, you can go to 2022 Documentation following this link.

After downloading the 161-MB zipped file, we just need to unzip it and put the unzipped file (VS22MORT.DUSMCPUB_r20240307, ~2.5 GB) in a folder that is convenient to access.

Since the microdata we are using is publicly available, certain geographic details—such as state, county, or major city of residence—are omitted to preserve confidentiality. This means fields like state and county of residence have been removed in the downloaded file. But the data still contains sufficient detail to support analysis at the national level.

NCHS mortality microdata is in fixed-width-file format, where each field (or column) has a designated width for consistent data alignment. For more information on reading and processing these files, refer to our previous post that discusses how to read and process these files, which covers methods for handling fixed-width data formats effectively.

We will need two fields in the mortality data for analyzing causes of death:

  • Resident Status: field location = 20, field size = 1 (i.e., this field is 1-character wide). This field can have values 1, 2, 3, or 4; the value 4 corresponds to ‘FOREIGN RESIDENTS’, which we need to exclude in our analysis.

  • ICD Code: field location = 146-149, field size = 4 (i.e., this field is 4-character wide). This field is for ICD-10 codes.

More information about fields is avaiable in 2022 Documentation.

Crosswalk between ICD-10 and rankable causes (from CDC WONDER)

As we mentioned in the introduction, we will need the crosswalk between ICD-10 and rankable causes, which was created in a previous post. This crosswalk will help us map the ICD-10 codes to rankable causes.

Most of the ICD-10 codes in the crosswalk are 3-character long (such as A01), each of which is actually for a group of ICD-10 codes. But there are some special cases in the crosswalk: 10 ICD-10 codes that have two more characters, such as A85.2.

Also, in the mortality data, the dots in the ICD-10 codes are not used. We will remove the dots in ICD-10 codes in the crosswalk dataframe and also create two columns to tract these two types of ICD-10 codes, i.e., 3-char ICD and 4-char ICD.

crosswalk_icd10_vs_causes <- read_csv("data/crosswalk_icd10_vs_causes.csv", show_col_types = FALSE)  %>% 
  select(icd_code = icd10, cause, cause_id) %>% 
  # remove the dot in some ICD-10 codes
  mutate(icd_code = str_replace(icd_code, "\\.", "")) %>% 
  mutate(icd_3char = if_else(str_length(icd_code)==3, icd_code, NA),
         icd_4char = if_else(str_length(icd_code)==4, icd_code, NA) ,
         .before = 2) %>% 
  glimpse()

Top 10 Leading Causes of Death

We partition the mortality data based on the condition if the ICD-10 code is in the 4-char ICD list or not. For rows with ICD-10 codes in the 4-char ICD list, those rows will left join the crosswalk using the key Icd_code. On the other hand, for rows with ICD-10 codes not in the 4-char ICD list, we get the first 3 characters in the ICD-10 code, check if the 3-char code in in the 3-char ICD list, and then left join the crosswalk by the key icd_3char.

Then we bind the rows from the two partitions, count the number of cases for each death cause, and find the top 10 leading causes.

leading_causes_2022 <- bind_rows(
  mort_2022 %>% 
    filter(icd_code %in% crosswalk_4_char_lst) %>% 
    left_join( crosswalk_icd10_vs_causes %>% select(icd_code, cause), by = "icd_code") ,
  
  mort_2022 %>% 
    filter(!(icd_code %in% crosswalk_4_char_lst)) %>% 
    mutate(icd_3char = str_sub(icd_code, 1L, 3L)) %>% 
    filter(icd_3char %in% crosswalk_3_char_lst) %>% 
    left_join( crosswalk_icd10_vs_causes %>% select(2,4), by = "icd_3char") %>% 
    filter(!is.na(cause))
) %>% 
  count(cause) %>% 
  top_n(10, n) %>% 
  arrange(desc(n)) %>% 
  mutate(rank = 1:10, .before = 1)

Summary

We began with the 2022 NCHS mortality microdata and, using a crosswalk to link ICD-10 codes to rankable causes of death, mapped each specific ICD-10 code to broader, standardized cause categories. This approach enabled us to compile and rank the top 10 leading causes of death in 2022 in the United States.

This analysis demonstrates the power of NCHS mortality microdata in uncovering detailed insights into mortality patterns. By facilitating an in-depth look at specific causes of death, this data is useful for researchers, policymakers, and public health officials who are working to reduce preventable deaths, allocate resources more effectively, and ultimately improve population health outcomes.

This post also outlines a framework for several potential follow-up analyses using mortality data. For instance, we could examine how leading causes of death vary across demographic factors like age, gender, race, and ethnicity, helping to reveal disparities and target interventions accordingly. Additionally, we could explore trends over time to observe shifts in mortality patterns, providing insight into the effectiveness of public health interventions, lifestyle changes, and emerging health threats.

Other follow-up analyses include age adjustment, which accounts for changes in population age structure, offering a clearer view of mortality rate shifts that aren’t solely driven by an aging population. Geographic analysis, where possible (e.g., using Restricted-Use Vital Statistics Data), could identify regional differences in mortality that may relate to local factors like healthcare access, socioeconomic conditions, or environmental risks.

For this analysis, we used R and Python, but the methods and workflow can be adapted to other analytical languages, such as SAS.
